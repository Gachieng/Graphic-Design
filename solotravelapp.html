Product Design (UI/UX)

Travel for 1 Solo Traveler app

Travel One Solo travelers need a convenient digital platform for comparing, tailoring and booking travel itineraries so that they can have a seamless travel experience. How might we personalise the travel booking process and connect solo travelers with experiences tailored specifically for them?

MY ROLE

  • UX design
  • UX research

SCOPE

  • Overall: 8+ weeks
  • Discovery & Research: 2+ weeks
  • Design & testing: 6 weeks

PROCESS

  • Research, competitive analysis, ideation, prototyping, usability testing, evaluation, UI design

TOOLS

  • Figma

The Problem

There is a gap in the market for a travel booking app that offers
solo traveller friendly packages without the common drawbacks.

Background Research

Solo travel has been steadily on the rise in the past two decades with more people choosing to travel alone. Some contributing factors to the popularization of solo travel are:

  • The rise of social media travel influencers popularizing the image of solo travel to make it seem enjoyable and more attainable to
  • People feeling motivated to see the world without waiting for others’ availability.
  • The increase in remote work options post COVID has given people more flexibility to be able to work and travel at the same time.
  • The rise of the digital nomad culture has driven governments to make visa provisions to accommodate these kinds of slow travelers who usually travel solo.

    • Solo travel has also had a negative stigma for the longest time with it being viewed as a practice for loners. But views are changing and now people have a more positive perception of it. Travel for One can successfully fill the market gap for solo travellers looking for a targeted travel booking app by creating an app that:

  • provides easy comparison between itineraries with built-in flexibility options
  • gives optional opportunities to socialise or spend time alone.
  • has no single supplement penalties for solo travellers.

    • Competitive Analysis

    Flashpack

    Flash pack is a solo travel company for travelers in their 30s and 40s. Focusing on an age group as a niche is helpful for aspiring travellers because it helps them to know what type of people to expect on their trip. It also helps them to connect with people in a similar life stage. Travel for One can offer age based trips to appeal to multiple age groups and appeal to a wider section of the market.

    Flash pack prides itself on offering unique travel experiences but needs to perfect other details such as; satisfactory accommodation and more inclusive meal package in order to justify the higher cost of the travel experiences they offer. Travel for one can capitalise on this weakness by including more meals in the travel packages to give travellers a more inclusive experience.

    The user interface on the view all trips page is a bit cluttered because it shows all available trips and the search filter section next to each other. A cleaner interface that reduces the trips shown and lets the search filter function show available trips for the users preferences could be more ideal.

    Travel for One can focus on creating a cleaner UI that is less cluttered, and incorporating a community building feature to appeal to solo travellers needs.

    Intrepid

    Intrepid offers small group travel to its customers, 50% of whom travel solo. The small group travel appeals to solo travelers as they are able to easily connect with each other.

    Having a dedicated section on their website that recommends the best trips for solo travelers makes it easy for those traveling solo to connect with each other. However, this section is not easy to find. Travel for One being a solo traveler app can make it easy for solo travellers to readily find information relevant to them.

    According to my research, providing options to choose the desired activity level and accommodation type which Intrepid offers, is also a huge plus for solo travellers. Travel for One can also benefit from having a similar feature.

    However, since Intrepid doesn’t solely cater to solo travellers, they may not be the go to travel company for solo travellers. This leaves room for competitors like Flashback or Travel for One to appeal to solo travellers more.

    The optional single supplement offered by intrepid gives an option to have your own room but is a deterrent for solo travellers as it represents an added cost that can be avoided.

    Travel for One can avoid the single supplement cost altogether as a competitive advantage in the solo traveller market.

    The Users: User Personas, Task flows

    After conducting background research, doing a competitive analysis, and going through reviews from actual solo travellers, I was able to better understand the potential users for the Travel for One booking app and what their expectations would be.
    These insights informed the creation of user personas to help guide the wire framing for the Travel for One app.

    Exploration and Ideation: IA and Wireframes

    From researching competitor websites and solo travelers' needs and pain points, I knew that I wanted the Travel for One app to contain a number of features to make the user experience pleasant and to ensure that the website meets user expectations.

  • A filtering feature that allowed for trips to be tailored based on destination type, age group or activity type to allow for personalised use of the interface and easy sorting through available trips.
  • A comparison tool for easily comparing between different trips.
  • An option for users to connect with other travellers before or after trips to aid in community building and reduce the anxiety solo travellers face before travel.
  • A simple clean interface that makes it easy for users to find information and navigate through the website.

    • Incorporating these features and other insights gained through researching competitor websites guided my wireframe creation process.

    Usability Testing

    After creating wireframes to get visuals for my initial ideas for the app, I created an interactive Mid-fidelity prototype to conduct some remote and in-person usability tests.

    With the help of two volunteers, I got to identify any potential usability issues within the app so that I could make the necessary iterations. I used Google meet and in person meetings to conduct the usability tests.

    I presented the participants with the following task scenarios and asked them to think out loud as they worked through them as I took notes and asked follow up questions.

    Scenario: You have just opened the app and you want to get a good idea about what it offers and its features then carry out some tasks on the app.
  • Task 1: Go through the onboarding process and describe your experience logging in and share any insights you gain about what the app offers and how it functions.
  • Task 2: Find what trips are available for booking on the app.
  • Task 3: Create a travel booking on the app
  • Task 4: Find some travel tips on the app
  • Task 5: Compare two trips that you’re interested in on the app
  • Task 6: Connect with other solo travelers on the app

    • Incorporating these features and other insights gained through researching competitor websites guided my wireframe creation process.

    Usability Test Results & Affinity Mapping

    After the usability test, I created affinity maps to organize user feedback and the notes I had taken during the tests.

    The feedback I received from the usability tests helped me iterate the UI for the app to make it more intuitive, clutter-free and easy to navigate which I will delve into more in the design section.

    User Interface Design

    The logic behind the Travel One app name wasn't clear that it's meant for solo travellers, so I made it a bit more obvious by changing it from Travel One to 'Travel for One'.

    The design for Travel for One started off as a website, but from research and the usability test feedback, I resolved to change Travel for One to a mobile app to allow for more a personalised, more intuitive user experience.

    During usability testing, I received feedback that the compare trips button and feature was not clear or obvious. Both users couldn’t identify it easily. It was only identifiable after spending some time on the app. I decluttered the trips page by removing the reviews section and adding micro text next to the check boxes as well as at the top of the page to give users clues for the comparison feature.

    There was also feedback about the contact us option. One of the users preferred a floating action button that allowed for chatting with the company in realtime on the app for quick help on any questions they may have as they navigated through the app. I incorporated this feature as I felt it would be within easy reach for users.

    I also noted that the connect feature for users to talk to other solo travelers before or after trips to build community or ask questions would be better as an in-app chat forum instead of requiring them to create an account. It also minimizes their commitment to the app while giving them the value of insights they seek to help them make travel decisions. These were some of the major changes I made in the design based on user feedback.

    What Working on Travel One Taught Me

    I learned that users like a clutter-free, simple intuitive interface that helps them access resources and information they need with minimal effort. Finding ways to align the conceptual model of the design with the mental models that users have, made the Travel for One app more likely to appeal to future users. It also ensured that users would face minimal friction navigating the app.

    Keeping user needs and pain points in mind as I came up with the design for the app helped me to keep the user at the centre of the design decisions and process. It also guided the prioritization of features. I learned that product design is an iterative process that ensures your design evolves as you learn more about your user.

    I got to understand the importance of background research and usability testing to help me put myself in the shoes of the user and empathize with them while understanding their motivations and addressing these through the design process. I look forward to taking this approach in my future designs.
